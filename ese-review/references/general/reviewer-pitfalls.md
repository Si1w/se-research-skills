# Reviewer Pitfalls — Biases to Avoid

A reviewer's job is to protect the community from weak science. But reviewers themselves are prone to systematic biases that distort the review. This reference catalogues the most common ones. Run a self-check against this list before submitting the report (Step 6).

The default disposition for ese-review is **skeptical** — that is a feature, not a bias. The pitfalls below are different: they are biases that produce *wrong* skepticism (rejecting good work) or *wrong* leniency (accepting weak work), not calibrated skepticism.

## Common Reviewer Biases

| Bias | Symptom | Counter |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Hypercriticism** | Inflating minor issues into critical weaknesses; treating every flaw as fatal | Distinguish critical (rejection-worthy on its own) from major (fixable in revision) from minor (line-level). If every item is critical, the review is uncalibrated. |
| **Confirmation bias** | Only finding evidence that supports your initial reaction (accept or reject) | After the first read, deliberately look for evidence against your initial impression. If you came in skeptical, look for genuine strengths. |
| **Preference projection** | Penalizing the authors for not using *your* preferred method | Ask "can this method answer the research question" — not "what would I have done". |
| **Paradigm bias** | Judging qualitative work by quantitative standards (or vice versa) | Match the evaluation criteria to the paper's research paradigm. Use the qualitative/quantitative/literature-review standards appropriately. |
| **Prestige bias** | Relaxing standards for authors from top labs; or tightening them out of resentment | Evaluate the paper as if author identity were unknown. |
| **Novelty bias** | Undervaluing replications, negative results, and tool papers | A well-executed replication of a contested result is a genuine contribution. So is a tool that the community will use. |
| **Length bias** | Equating page count or word count with rigor | Evaluate content density. A tight 8-page paper can outclass a padded 14-page one. |
| **Language bias** | Conflating "non-native English needs polishing" with "research is weak" | Report language issues as minor (or as a separate writing-quality flag). Do not let them contaminate the methodological assessment. |
| **Anchoring on the abstract** | Forming a verdict from the abstract and reading the rest to confirm it | Read the methodology and results before deciding. The abstract often overclaims; the body often underdelivers — both directions of mismatch matter. |
| **Recency bias** | Penalizing the paper for missing literature published *after* its submission | Check submission/preprint dates. Required references are those available at submission time. |

## Principles of Constructive Criticism

Even in a harsh review, criticism is more useful when it follows three rules:

1. **Specific, not vague**: "The causal inference in Section 3.2, paragraph 4, lacks control for project size" beats "methodology has problems".
2. **Problem + reason + suggested fix**: every criticism should answer *what* is wrong, *why* it undermines the contribution, and *how* it could be addressed.
3. **Distinguish required from suggested**: which changes block acceptance, which are merely "nice to have". Mixing them makes the revision letter unactionable.

These rules do not soften the review — they sharpen it. A vague criticism is easy to deflect. A specific one with a fix is not.

## Self-Check Before Submitting the Review

Run through this checklist after drafting the report:

- [ ] Is any criticism stronger than the evidence I have? (Hypercriticism check.)
- [ ] Did I look for genuine strengths, even if briefly? (Confirmation bias check.)
- [ ] Am I criticising the paper for not being the paper I would have written? (Preference projection check.)
- [ ] Did I apply the right empirical standards for the paper's research type? (Paradigm bias check.)
- [ ] Did I let language quality contaminate the methodology assessment? (Language bias check.)
- [ ] Could a reasonable author act on every "Major Issue" I raised? If not, the criticism is not actionable.
- [ ] Did I separate required revisions from suggested improvements?

A review that survives this self-check is harsh, fair, and useful. A review that fails it is harsh and useless.
