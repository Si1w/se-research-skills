# Introduction

## Audience
- First-tier venue referees may not be expert in your area.
- The introduction must give them a good feel for what you are proposing.
- Explain with clear and compelling evidence why they should care.
- Referees may be reviewing 15–25 other papers in a short period.

## Contribution Statement
- End the introduction with a short statement of contributions.
- Use an enumerated list (not bullets).
- This is essentially: "this is why you should accept my paper."
- Refine and re-refine until it is as tight and compelling as possible.

## First Impressions
- By the time referees finish reading contributions, they may have already decided accept/reject.
- If they decided to accept: reading the rest to check for unexpected issues.
- If they decided to reject: reading the rest to find more things to dislike.
- Not all referees are like this, but cater for those who are.

## The Paper's "Envelope"
The first impression is made by the paper's envelope and boxouts:
- **Envelope**: title, abstract, introduction, conclusions, reference list
- **Boxouts**: equations, figures, tables — anything that stands out when flicking through the paper
