# Problem Formulation

## Purpose

This section gives the definitive, precise statement of the problem being solved. It establishes the formal framework that the rest of the paper builds on. Not every paper needs a separate problem formulation section — if the introduction does a good job and the problem is well-understood, a separate section may not be necessary.

## Format

- Use when the problem requires formal definitions, mathematical notation, or is complex enough that the introduction cannot fully specify it.
- Should be self-contained: a reader should be able to understand the problem from this section alone.
- Typically 0.5–1.5 pages.

## Required Elements

- **Formal problem statement**: Clear definitions of inputs, outputs, constraints, and objectives.
- **Notation**: If mathematical notation is needed, introduce it here and use it consistently throughout the paper.
- **Scope**: What is in scope and what is not. Explicit boundaries prevent the referee from expecting something the paper does not deliver.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Tainted by solution**: The problem formulation must not discuss the proposed solution or how you plan to evaluate it. It describes the problem, nothing more. If you find yourself writing "we use..." or "our approach...", you have crossed into the solution section.
- **Fragmented (DeFrag violation)**: Bits of the problem formulation scattered across the introduction, background, and solution sections. The formulation here should be the definitive one — collect it all in one place.
- **Over-formalisation**: Introducing heavy notation for a problem that can be stated clearly in prose. Formalism should aid clarity, not obscure it.
- **Under-specification**: Leaving key terms undefined or ambiguous. Every technical term used in the problem statement should have a precise definition.

## Checklist

- [ ] Problem is stated formally and precisely
- [ ] Notation is introduced and consistent
- [ ] Scope is clearly bounded
- [ ] No discussion of the proposed solution
- [ ] No discussion of evaluation methodology
- [ ] All the problem formulation is here (not scattered across other sections)
- [ ] Definitions are precise and unambiguous
